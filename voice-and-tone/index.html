Voice and tone

Voice and tone is how we sound when we’re speaking or writing as the RCVS.

Our voice is our unique, distinctive personality. Our tone is how we adapt our voice for different situations, depending on who we’re talking to, what we’re talking about and what we want people to do.

Our voice remains the same, but our tone changes in order to be appropriate. Together, voice and tone help us communicate authentically and consistently. They’re vital for building positive relationships.

Voice

Our voice is compassionate, confident and calm.

1. Compassionate, but never indulgent.

We’re eager to listen, understand and help. We assume good intent and are supportive of our community, from the veterinary professions to the animal-owning public. We’re respectful and kind without being gushing.

If concerns are raised about you, it can be a worrying time. The information here outlines our process for dealing with concerns.

If concerns are raised about you, we’ll do everything we can to ease your nerves. The information here outlines our process for dealing with concerns in order to put your mind at rest.

2. Confident, but never bullish.

Our expertise and our status give us confidence, but not arrogance or bullishness. We ground our confidence in facts, experience and knowledge.

We enhance society through improved animal health and welfare.

We enhance society by ensuring the highest standards in animal health and welfare.

3. Calm, but never listless.

We are reliable and steadfast. That doesn’t mean that we’re dull or lacking in energy.

If you believe that someone is working as a veterinary surgeon when not qualified to do so, we would like to hear from you.

If you believe that someone is working as a veterinary surgeon when not qualified to do so, you may choose to notify us.

Tone

Our tone has two spectrums: from lighthearted to serious, and from warm to matter-of-fact.

Where content sits on this matrix depends on the context. Certain types of content will gravitate towards a particular point. For example, a case study about misconduct will sit at the serious and matter-of-fact ends of the scales, whereas an Instagram post about a vet nurse and their dog will be more lighthearted and warm.

When we want to reassure and encourage

People visiting our website and other channels often need to be reassured and encouraged. They could be worried, stressed or confused. When this is our goal, we want our tone to be towards the serious and warm ends of the spectrums.

How to achieve this:

  • Use positive language
  • Be empathetic
  • Talk about emotions as well as facts
  • Speak directly to people, using conversational language

Your practice will be working hard to offer the best service they can, so please be patient and understanding during this difficult time.

Your practice is very busy during this time. Your patience is required to ensure your concerns are heard.

When we want to guide and instruct

Helping vets navigate the regulatory process means striking a balance between being informative and empathising with the challenges. The right tone for this is on the serious end of the lighthearted > serious scale and in the middle of the warm > matter-of-fact scale.

How to achieve this:

  • Use clear, concise language
  • Be idiomatic: use familiar but not informal language

Apply to register early to avoid delays. When we receive your application, we’ll contact you to set a date for a video registration appointment.

To avoid any delay to when you can start work, we recommend that you apply to register with all required documentation, as soon as possible. You will receive further information during the processing of your application regarding a video registration appointment date.

When we want to inspire and engage

Sometimes the RCVS needs to show people how veterinary science is advancing and get them excited about it. To get this tone right, find a balance between serious and lighthearted while leaning on the slightly more warm end of the warm > matter-of-fact spectrum.

How to achieve this:

  • Use action-oriented language
  • Speak directly to the user and their experience of the world
  • Avoid overly serious or formal constructions

The RCVS Fellowship creates a thriving learning society for vets. We invite people to be fellows when they show exceptional commitment to enhancing the veterinary profession and landscape.

Specifically, the RCVS Fellowship seeks to fulfil this aim by creating a thriving learned society. Its prestige is built upon its ability to attract veterinary surgeons whose activities demonstrate excellence across the entire veterinary landscape and who have distinguished themselves on account of their ongoing commitment to using their experience and knowledge to enhance the profession further.
