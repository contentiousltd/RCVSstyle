Bold, italics and underlining

Bold

We use bold – sparingly – to highlight the most important part of a message:

The deadline for applications is noon on 14 July.

In print content, you can make web addresses, email addresses, hashtags and other social media identifiers bold, if you need to clearly show the reader where to go or what action to take:

#YourKindOfVets

info@rcvs.org.uk

rcvs.org.uk

Don't overdo it, as it can distract the reader and make content less readable. And don't make punctuation bold, unless the whole sentence is.

Italics

We italicise the titles of books, journals and publications:

The RCVS Code of Professional Conduct for Veterinary Surgeons is set out in full below.

If the title includes “The” or “A” at the start, italicise that as well:

The 2019 Survey of the Veterinary Nursing Profession is now available.

But use quotation marks rather than italics if you're talking about an article within a larger publication or a chapter in a book, in order to differentiate between the two:

Chapter 18 of the Code of Professional Conduct for Veterinary Surgeons, “Delegation to veterinary nurses”, covers this.

We use italics to emphasise words within a sentence where they make the meaning clearer and help to make the point. Keep this kind of emphasis to a minimum.

The word the appears twice in this sentence.

Use italics for species names in Latin:

The black rat (Rattus rattus) is very common in UK cities.

We also italicise lesser-known foreign words and phrases, though we generally avoid using those. If it's a commonly used foreign word or phrase, there's no need to italicise:

It’s a cliché, but it’s also true.

Underlining

We don’t underline words unless they’re links. Underlined words that are not links are confusing online and look messy in print.
