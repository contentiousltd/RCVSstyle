Accessibility

Providing equal access to our content to everyone, no matter what their needs are, is the right thing to do. In the UK, 1 in 5 people have a long-term illness or impairment, or are disabled. And most people will be temporarily or situationally impaired at some times, such as by a broken wrist, a headache, bright sunlight or a lack of headphones. We don’t want to exclude these people.

Accessibility also benefits everyone. The principles of accessible content are closly aligned with best practice in user experience. All users appreciate things being easy to understand and action.

Assistive technology

Bear in mind that some people, for all sorts of reasons, may be using screen readers or other assistive technology to consume our content. To ensure our content works for them, we aim to meet – as a minimum – the requirements of WCAG 2.1 Level AA. This means meeting all the requirements that are Level A and Level AA.

Images

Alt text

Alt text, sometimes referred to as alt tags or alt descriptions, is the written copy that appears in place of an image on a webpage if the image doesn’t load on the user’s screen. It tells people what’s contained in the image, is vital for screen readers and helps search engines crawl and rank your website.

Add alt text to images, including infographics, so that anyone who can’t see the image can still understand and follow the content.

For decorative images that are included for purely aesthetic reasons and don’t add meaning or context to the content, use an empty alt tag (alt=““) in order not to add audio clutter for those using screen readers.

This decision tree will help you decide whether or not you need to add alt text to an image.

Your alt text should be specific and descriptive. Let’s look at good and bad versions of alt text for the below image.

Three vet students from the Department of Veterinary Medicine at the University of Cambridge assessing a horse during their field work.

A group of women in front of a horse next to a blue barn.

Including details about where the vet students are from will give the image more meaning and depth and are specifics that Google would need to properly index the image if it’s in a blog post about students at the University of Cambridge.

The poor example is not descriptive enough and does not help identify who, where, and why.

Text in images

Avoid using text in images. Text should be HTML whenever possible.

Captions and transcripts for audio and visual

When you use an image or a video you should always include a plain language description of the content if it is essential to understanding.

Make sure you provide a captions file for pre-recorded video that has speech or other audio. Auto-generated Youtube or Vimeo captions aren’t acceptable in their raw state. Wherever possible, include a full transcript so people with a visual impairment can access the content equally.

When using audio content, provide a transcript to allow those with auditory impairments to access the content.

More detailed guidance can be found in Planning Audio and Video Media on the Web Accessibility Initiative.

Hyperlinks

Link text should make sense even without the link. We don't use “the page”, “click here” or “click this link”.

Add hyperlinks to descriptive text about the information users will find if they follow the link.

If clicking on a link will open a PDF or other document, or start a download, make that clear to the user.

Directional language

Avoid directional language. Examples of directional language include phrases such as “comment below”. Often this assumes the reader is not using assistive technology to consume content.

Rather than using directional language, use the following tactics:

  1. Use headings and whitespace to create visual hierarchy and help guide the user.
  2. Group related information, because people assume that things that are next to each other are connected.
  3. Hyperlink keywords that tell you what’s on the other side of the link.

Readability

As a general rule of thumb, long sentences that contain a lot of words are harder to read than shorter sentences.

Similarly, words that have multiple syllables are harder to read than shorter words.

The Hemingway app is a helpful free online tool for checking how readable, and therefore accessible, your writing is.

Tables

Tables can be useful for displaying tabular data in a digestible way. Don’t use tables to present lists or other content in a certain layout.

If tables are used inappropriately, they can be really bad for accessibility. Assistive technology, such as screen readers won’t be able to associate the table contents with the headings, so people accessing the content with these tools, won’t be able to understand the information.

Before you add a table to a web page, consider if there’s a more accessible way that you can present that information, for example as a bulleted list.

In order to be accessible tables need HTML markup that indicates header cells and data cells and defines their relationship.

Structured content

Page structure helps guide users to valuable information.

Apply headings (H1s, H2s and H3s) in nested order, so that H2s are related to the H1, and H3s are related to H2s.

  • H1 (Page topic) Introductory text
  • H2 (Subtopic) One idea
  • H3 (Subtopic of previous) One idea

All headings should be in sentence case and should ideally be no more than five words.

This guidance draws on the 18F style guide and the NHS content style guide.

Link text should make sense even without the link. We don't use 'the page', 'click here' or 'click this link' to describe links.
